FAQ

Common Questions About Renting in Australia

Is my rental lease normal in Australia?
LeaseLand reviews your lease against standard practices and legal requirements in your state. Many leases contain clauses that are technically legal but unusual, while others include illegal terms. Our AI flags both so you can negotiate or dispute informed.
What are the bond rules in Australia?
Each Australian state has its own bond authority (e.g. NSW Fair Trading, RTBA in VIC). Generally, bonds are capped at 4 weeks' rent (unfurnished) or 6 weeks (furnished, in some states). Landlords must lodge the bond with the state authority within a set timeframe. LeaseLand explains exactly how bonds work in your state.
Can I break my lease as an international student?
Yes, but breaking a lease usually involves costs like re-letting fees, advertising, and rent until a new tenant is found. Most states cap these costs. You may also need to give notice — typically 14 to 28 days depending on your state and situation. LeaseLand explains the exact costs and notice periods for your state.
How do I get my bond back in NSW / VIC / QLD?
Bond return processes vary by state. In NSW, you apply via Rental Bonds Online. In VIC, via the RTBA. The landlord has a set timeframe to dispute claims. If they don't, your bond is returned automatically. LeaseLand provides state-specific bond recovery guidance to help you get your full deposit back.
What can I do if my landlord won't do repairs?
Landlords must maintain properties to a minimum standard under each state's tenancy laws. If urgent repairs aren't done, tenants in many states can arrange repairs up to a certain dollar amount and be reimbursed. For non-urgent issues, you may need to apply to your state's tribunal. LeaseLand can guide you through your specific situation.
Is LeaseLand legal advice?
No. LeaseLand provides general information and educational guidance only. It is not a substitute for professional legal advice. We always recommend consulting a qualified professional, such as a tenancy advocate or community legal centre, for your specific situation.
